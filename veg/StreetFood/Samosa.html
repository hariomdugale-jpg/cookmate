Samosa

India's Crispiest, Most Loved Snack

1. Make the Pastry Dough
In a large bowl, combine maida, ajwain (carom seeds), a generous pinch of salt and 3 tablespoons of oil or ghee. Rub the fat into the flour with your fingertips until the mixture resembles coarse breadcrumbs — this step is called "moyan" and is the secret to a flaky, crispy pastry shell. The fat must be completely worked in before water is added. Add cold water little by little and knead into a firm, stiff dough — much stiffer than chapati dough. A soft dough will give you oily, limp samosas. Knead for 5 minutes until smooth. Cover with a damp cloth and rest for 20–30 minutes.
2. Make the Potato-Pea Filling
Boil potatoes until completely cooked, peel and mash roughly — leave some chunky texture. Heat 1 tablespoon oil in a pan. Add cumin seeds and let them splutter. Add coarsely crushed coriander seeds and fry for 20 seconds. Add grated ginger and finely chopped green chillies; cook for 1 minute. Add the green peas and cook for 2 minutes until just tender. Add the mashed potato and mix well. Season with amchur, red chilli powder, garam masala and salt. Fold in chopped coriander. The filling must be completely dry — any moisture will make the pastry soggy and cause it to burst during frying. Taste and adjust spices. Cool completely before filling.
3. Roll & Shape the Samosas
Divide the rested dough into equal balls. On a lightly oiled surface (not floured), roll each ball into a thin oval — about 15–18 cm long and 3–4 mm thick. Cut the oval in half to get two semi-circles. Take one semi-circle and form a cone by folding and sealing the straight edge with a little water — pinch firmly so it is sealed completely with no gaps. Fill the cone generously with the cooled potato filling — do not overfill or it will burst. Press the filling down gently. Seal the open top edge firmly by pressing together and pleating the seam neatly. The seal must be airtight. Repeat with all the dough.
4. Fry Low & Slow for Maximum Crispiness
Heat oil in a deep heavy pan to 150–160°C — this lower temperature is the key to a samosa with a blistered, shatteringly crispy shell. If the oil is too hot, the outside browns fast but the pastry stays soft and doughy inside. Slide 4–5 samosas gently into the oil. Fry on medium-low heat for 12–15 minutes, turning occasionally, until they are a deep, even golden brown all over. Do not rush by raising the heat. The long slow fry drives out all moisture from the pastry and gives that iconic blistered, flaky crunch. Drain on paper towels.
5. Serve Hot with Chutneys
Serve samosas piping hot immediately after frying — the crunch fades as they cool. Serve alongside green mint-coriander chutney and sweet tamarind-date chutney. For the full chaat experience, crush a samosa open on a plate and top with both chutneys, a spoonful of chole, chopped onion, fresh coriander, chaat masala and sev — this is called Samosa Chaat and it is a meal in itself. Whether eaten plain with cutting chai on a rainy afternoon or dressed up as chaat, samosa is India's most universally beloved snack.
