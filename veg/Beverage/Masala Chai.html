Masala Chai

India's Spiced Tea — Warm, Bold & Comforting

Steps (Click for details)

1. Crush the Spices & Boil Water
Lightly crush 2–3 green cardamom pods and a small thumb of fresh ginger using the back of a knife or a mortar and pestle — you want them bruised and cracked open, not powdered. This releases the essential oils far more effectively than whole or pre-ground spices. Add 1 cup of water to a small saucepan and drop in the crushed ginger and cardamom. Bring to a boil on medium heat and let it simmer for 1–2 minutes so the water absorbs the spice flavour fully before the tea and milk go in.
2. Add Tea & Sugar
Add 1 heaped teaspoon of CTC tea leaves per cup directly into the simmering spiced water. CTC tea is essential — its bold, malty flavour stands up to milk and spices far better than loose leaf or bag tea. Add sugar to taste now — adding sugar early allows it to meld into the chai rather than sitting on top. Stir and let the tea boil in the water for 1 minute on medium heat. The liquid will turn a deep reddish-brown. This decoction stage builds the base strength of the chai — a weak decoction will be washed out once the milk is added.
3. Add Milk & Bring to a Boil
Pour in full fat milk — roughly equal parts milk to water, or slightly more milk for a creamier chai. Stir to combine. Raise the heat to medium-high and bring the chai to a full rolling boil, watching it carefully. As it rises, it will foam up dramatically — this is the moment. Let it rise just to the brim of the pan, then lower the heat immediately or lift the pan off briefly to settle it. This rise-and-settle is the technique used at every chai stall in India and it is what gives chai its characteristic body and frothy top. Repeat the boil-and-settle once more for an even richer, more integrated flavour.
4. Strain & Pour
Hold a fine mesh strainer over your cup or glass. Pour the chai through the strainer in one confident pour — a high pour from the saucepan creates a beautiful froth on top and cools the chai to the perfect drinking temperature. The strainer catches the tea leaves, ginger pieces and cardamom husks. Tap the strainer lightly to release all the liquid. The chai in the cup should be a warm golden-brown colour, steaming and topped with a thin layer of froth. If it looks pale, it needed more tea or a longer boil. Taste before serving — adjust sweetness if needed, though this is difficult to fix once poured.
