Spaghetti Aglio e Olio

Italy's Most Elegant 6-Ingredient Pasta

1. Boil the Pasta in Heavily Salted Water
Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. Salt the water generously — it should taste like the sea. This is the only chance to season the pasta itself, so do not hold back. Add the spaghetti and cook until just al dente — usually 1–2 minutes less than the package instructions. The pasta will finish cooking in the sauce, so pulling it out slightly underdone is intentional. Before draining, reserve at least one full cup of the starchy pasta water — this cloudy, salty, starchy liquid is the secret ingredient that binds the entire dish and creates the silky sauce. Do not forget it.
2. Slice the Garlic — Thin & Even
Peel a generous amount of garlic — for two servings, 6–8 large cloves is not too many. Slice each clove as thinly and evenly as possible using a sharp knife. Uniform slices are important because they will all cook at the same rate — uneven pieces will have some burning before others are done. Do not use a garlic press or minced garlic here; sliced garlic gives a milder, nuttier flavour that blooms into the oil beautifully rather than turning sharp and bitter. Set the sliced garlic aside. This dish has very few ingredients — the quality and preparation of each one determines everything.
3. Toast the Garlic in Olive Oil — Low & Slow
In a large, wide pan, pour a very generous amount of extra virgin olive oil — more than you think is needed. Heat on medium-low. Add the sliced garlic and chilli flakes. Cook slowly, stirring often, for 4–6 minutes. The garlic should turn pale golden, not brown — the moment garlic goes dark it turns bitter and the dish is ruined. Watch it carefully; it goes from perfect to burnt in seconds. The oil should be gently bubbling around the garlic, infusing with its flavour. The kitchen will smell incredible. Remove from heat if the garlic is colouring too fast — residual heat will continue cooking it.
4. Emulsify with Pasta Water
With the garlic still in the pan on low heat, add a ladleful of the reserved starchy pasta water — it will hiss and steam vigorously. Stir and swirl the pan to combine the oil and water into a single emulsified, creamy-looking sauce. This step is the technique that separates a restaurant-quality aglio e olio from a greasy one — the starch in the pasta water binds the oil and water together. Add the drained al dente spaghetti directly into the pan. Toss vigorously, adding more pasta water a little at a time if needed, until every strand is glossy, coated and the sauce clings to the pasta rather than sitting in a pool at the bottom.
5. Plate & Finish Immediately
Remove the pan from heat. Add a generous handful of finely chopped fresh flat-leaf parsley and toss through — the parsley adds freshness and colour that lifts the dish. Taste and adjust salt. Crack plenty of fresh black pepper over the top. Twirl the spaghetti into a tall mound in a warm bowl using tongs or a fork. Drizzle a final thread of your best extra virgin olive oil over the top — raw olive oil at the end adds a completely different fruity, peppery dimension. Finish with grated Parmesan if desired, though purists argue the dish needs nothing else. Serve immediately — aglio e olio waits for no one.
