1. Roast the Baingan
Wash and dry the eggplant. Rub it all over with oil. Place directly over a gas flame on medium heat. Roast turning with tongs every 2–3 minutes for 15–20 minutes until the skin is completely charred and the inside is soft and collapsed. This charring gives Baingan Bharta its signature smoky flavour — it cannot be replicated any other way.
2. Peel & Mash the Baingan
Place the roasted baingan in a bowl and let it cool for 10 minutes. Peel off all the charred skin under running water or on a board — it should come off easily. Remove the stem. Mash the soft pulp with a fork until coarsely mashed. Do not over-mash — a little texture makes the bharta more rustic and authentic.
3. Fry Onions & Chilli
Heat mustard oil or regular oil in a pan on medium-high heat until hot. Add finely chopped onions and slit green chillies. Fry for 5–6 minutes until the onions turn deep golden brown. The well-fried onions form the flavour backbone of the bharta.
4. Add Ginger-Garlic & Tomatoes
Add ginger-garlic paste and cook for 2 minutes until the raw smell disappears. Add finely chopped tomatoes and cook on medium heat for 5–6 minutes until completely soft, mushy and the oil separates from the masala.
5. Add Spices
Add red chilli powder, turmeric, coriander powder and salt. Mix well and cook the masala for 2–3 minutes on medium heat until the spices are cooked and fragrant and oil rises to the surface of the masala.
6. Add the Mashed Baingan
Add the mashed roasted baingan pulp to the masala. Mix everything together thoroughly. Cook on medium heat for 8–10 minutes stirring occasionally so the baingan fully absorbs all the masala and the smoky flavour intensifies. The bharta should look dry and well combined.
7. Garnish & Serve
Sprinkle garam masala and a generous amount of fresh coriander leaves on top. Drizzle a little raw mustard oil for extra aroma if desired. Serve hot with roti, paratha, naan or steamed rice and dal.
