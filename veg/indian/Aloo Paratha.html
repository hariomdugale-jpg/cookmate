Aloo Paratha

Punjab's Ultimate Stuffed Flatbread

1. Make the Wheat Dough
In a large bowl, combine whole wheat atta with a pinch of salt, a pinch of ajwain and 1 teaspoon of oil. Mix well. Add warm water gradually and knead into a soft, smooth, pliable dough — softer than a roti dough but not sticky. Knead for 7–8 minutes until the dough is elastic and smooth. The dough should bounce back when poked. Drizzle a little oil on the surface, cover with a damp cloth or cling film and rest for 20–30 minutes. Resting relaxes the gluten and makes the dough easier to roll without tearing. Do not skip this step.
2. Prepare the Aloo Filling
Boil potatoes until completely cooked through — no hard centres. Peel while still warm and mash thoroughly until completely smooth with no lumps at all. Even a single lump will tear the paratha when rolling. To the mashed potato add finely chopped green chilli, grated ginger, finely chopped onion (if using), chopped fresh coriander, red chilli powder, amchur, garam masala, cumin seeds and salt. Mix everything very well. Taste and adjust — the filling should be bold, well-seasoned and tangy from the amchur. The filling must be completely dry; wet filling tears the dough. Divide into equal portions the same size as your dough balls.
3. Stuff & Seal the Paratha
Divide the rested dough into equal-sized balls. Take one dough ball and roll it into a small circle of about 10–12 cm diameter. Place one portion of the aloo filling in the centre. Bring the edges of the dough together over the filling, gathering and pinching to completely enclose it — like a filled dumpling. Press the sealed ball gently with your palm to flatten slightly. Dust lightly with dry flour. Now gently and evenly roll it out into a flat circle of about 18–20 cm. Apply even, gentle pressure from the centre outward — if rolled too hard in one spot the filling will burst through. If a small hole appears, patch it immediately with a pinch of dough.
4. Cook on the Tawa with Butter
Heat a flat iron tawa or heavy non-stick pan on medium-high heat until very hot. Place the rolled paratha on the dry tawa. Cook for 60–90 seconds until small bubbles appear on the surface and the underside has light golden spots. Flip. Apply a generous knob of butter on the cooked side — it will sizzle and melt into the paratha immediately. Cook the second side for 60–90 seconds. Flip again and apply butter on this side too. Press gently with a spatula to ensure even contact with the tawa. Cook until both sides are deeply golden with dark spots and the paratha is cooked through. The butter should be deeply absorbed — do not be shy with it.
5. Finish with Ghee & Serve
Remove the paratha from the tawa and immediately brush or pour a small spoon of ghee on top while piping hot — this is the finishing touch that gives Punjabi paratha its legendary richness and aroma. Stack finished parathas and keep covered in a clean cloth to stay warm and soft. Serve with a large dollop of thick, cold fresh curd alongside tangy achaar (pickle) — this hot-cold, rich-sharp contrast is what makes aloo paratha a perfect meal. A glass of cold lassi on the side is the Punjabi way. Eat the paratha while it is hot; nothing compares to a fresh-off-the-tawa aloo paratha.
